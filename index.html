Software Engineering Internship
Renovo Auto

java, android, adobe illustrator, ZeroMQ

A Renovo collaboration with their customer, the Stanford Dynamic Design Lab. Renovo supplied a high-power electric drivetrain to the research vehicle, a 1981 Delorean named MARTY.

I developed mobile applications that relayed telemetry data from the vehicle to the dashboard tablets, and to remote Android devices used to track vital vehicle data by Renovo engineers, such as battery temperatures and voltages.





I built the native applications using Java, and designed everything from scratch. I built all graphics elements in Adobe Illustrator. An Intel Edison was used to publish CAN data from an attached CAN card. I utilized the ZeroMQ Pub/Sub pattern to allow multipe Android devices to connect through a Wifi Direct connection.

Some early prototype designs.







And the final design showing most of the resulting data. A swipe on the lower screen reveals more detailed battery information.

Robotics
DART

simulation, dart, robotics


As a graduate student at Georgia Tech, I had the fortunate opportunity to work with a giant hardware platform, Golem Krang, which is a wheeled, inverted pendulum humanoid. I assisted in many aspects of the research projects conducted on this platform during my two years in the lab, including driver-level coding, control software, dynamics simulation, conducting experiment and hardware upgrades and repairs. In particular, my Masters thesis involved development of an Inverse Kinematics controller for Krang's arms, as well as installation of a perception system for target object detection and grasping through visual servo.

Portrait of Golem Krang.


A model of one of Krang's arms, with ZED Stereo Camera attached


An inverse kinematics formulation for the 7-DOF arms of the robot was applied and preliminary results are shown at the link below:

Optimizaiton-Based Inverse Kinematics Solution for a 7-DOF Arm

I utilized a depth camera for finding the 3D location of target objects to be grasped by the robot. This was essentially a visual-servo control problem, utilizing the above-developed controller for the grasping task.


A pipeline from the raw image to detecting objects of interest (in this case a tennis ball) to be grasped utilizing OpenCV, as well as a visual representation of the depth map built by the ZED Camera.


The full system working on hardware, tracking the tennis ball and moving the arm to a grasping position (sequence from top-left to bottom-right.


In addition to visual-servo, I was able to utilize the stereo cameras positional tracking capabilities to estimate the angle of the base link of the robot. The goal was to estimate the state of the link to compliment the noisy signal published by the IMU.
